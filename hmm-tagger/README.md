# HMM + Viterbi Tagger

*Baum, Petrie, and others, 1966–70s; Viterbi, 1967 · sequence labelling*

[`../pos-markov/`](../pos-markov/)'s own shortcomings section names the fix and
leaves it undone: "real taggers use HMMs, then neural models." This is that
fix. The baseline tagger looks at one word at a time — "rose" is tagged the
same way *everywhere*, because the tagger is a pure function of a word's
spelling, with no notion of what surrounds it. A **Hidden Markov Model**, decoded
with the **Viterbi algorithm**, tags the whole sentence at once, so an
ambiguous word gets resolved by its neighbours instead of by itself alone.

## The idea

An HMM POS tagger treats a sentence as being generated by an invisible walk
through tags, where each tag "emits" a word:

```
P(tags, words)  =  P(tag₁) · P(word₁ | tag₁)  ×  P(tag₂ | tag₁) · P(word₂ | tag₂)  ×  …
```

Two probability tables drive this:

- **Transition probabilities**, `P(tag | previous tag)` — how likely a
  Verb is to follow a Pronoun, a Noun to follow a Determiner, and so on. This
  is exactly a bigram Markov chain, just over tags instead of words.
- **Emission probabilities**, `P(word | tag)` — how likely a specific word
  is to show up, given the tag someone is currently "in". This is what lets
  the *same word* be reachable from more than one tag, at different
  likelihoods.

The tags themselves are "hidden" — you observe the words, not the tags — so
tagging means finding the tag sequence most likely to have produced the
sentence you actually see. Trying every possible tag sequence is exponential
in sentence length; the **Viterbi algorithm** finds the best one in linear
time using the same dynamic-programming trick as
[`../edit-distance/`](../edit-distance/)'s matrix: build up the best score
*ending at* each (position, tag) cell from the best scores at the previous
position, keeping a backpointer, and read the winning path off the end.

## Training data, honestly

This repository has no hand-annotated corpus, and building one is out of
scope for a zero-dependency project. So this tagger is trained two different
ways at once, and neither is hidden:

1. **Transition counts** come from auto-tagging the real sonnets with
   `pos-markov/`'s baseline tagger. Individual word-tags are sometimes wrong,
   but the *sequence* patterns of English survive the noise — a Pronoun really
   is followed by a Verb far more often than by a Conjunction, regardless of
   which specific words got mistagged along the way.
2. **Emission counts** come from the same auto-tagged corpus, **plus** a
   small, explicitly hand-written set of *ambiguity seeds* — a handful of
   classic ambiguous English words (`rose`, `light`, `still`) given a second
   possible tag with a hand-picked count. Without this, the model could never
   learn that "rose" is anything but a Noun: every single occurrence in the
   sonnets is the flower, and the tagger that generated the training labels
   is itself a deterministic function of spelling. This is not a trick to
   hide — it is the whole lesson. An HMM only disambiguates what its labelled
   data has actually shown it. Real taggers need real annotated corpora
   (the Penn Treebank, for instance) for exactly this reason.

## What the program builds

Two count tables, smoothed with Laplace (add-1) — the same technique
`naive-bayes/` uses, for the same reason: an unseen transition or emission
should get a small nonzero probability, not zero out the whole sentence.
Real counts from Shakespeare's sonnets (17,608 tokens):

```
Transition counts out of "Pronoun":        Transition counts out of "Determiner":
  Pronoun -> Noun         557                 Determiner -> Noun         1947
  Pronoun -> Verb         376                 Determiner -> Verb          197
  Pronoun -> Preposition  169                 Determiner -> Determiner    150
  Pronoun -> Determiner   136                 Determiner -> Pronoun       141
  Pronoun -> Adverb       116                 Determiner -> Preposition    78
```

Notice the shape of these two rows: after a **Determiner**, the corpus is
almost unanimous — Noun outnumbers Verb nearly 10 to 1. After a **Pronoun**,
Noun only edges out Verb 557 to 376, a much softer lean. That difference in
how *decisive* the context is turns out to be the whole story below.

## Worked example (real output): the same word, two contexts

```
$ node hmm-tagger/index.js corpora/sonnets-shakespeare.txt "they rose"
  word        baseline (pos-markov)   HMM + Viterbi
  they        Pronoun                 Pronoun
  rose        Noun                    Verb  <- differ

$ node hmm-tagger/index.js corpora/sonnets-shakespeare.txt "the rose is fair"
  word        baseline (pos-markov)   HMM + Viterbi
  the         Determiner              Determiner
  rose        Noun                    Noun
  is          Verb                    Verb
  fair        Noun                    Noun
```

The baseline tagger gives "rose" the same answer, Noun, in both sentences —
it cannot do otherwise; it never looks past the word itself. The HMM gives
two *different* answers, and both are right. Here is why, in the actual
numbers: the emission table gives `rose` a slightly *higher* probability
under Verb than under Noun (`P(rose|Verb) ≈ 0.00138` vs. `P(rose|Noun) ≈
0.00068`) — not because Verb is more common overall (it isn't: 1,894 Verb
tokens vs. 7,196 Noun tokens in the training data), but because the six
hand-seeded Verb occurrences of "rose" are a *larger share* of the smaller
Verb class than the six real Noun occurrences are of the much larger Noun
class. That small emission tilt toward Verb is enough to win after "they",
where the transition evidence (Pronoun &rarr; Noun vs. Verb) is close. It is
nowhere near enough to win after "the", where the transition evidence
(Determiner &rarr; Noun vs. Verb, 1947 to 197) is overwhelming. **Context
strong enough to override the word's own bias wins; context too weak does
not** — which is exactly what a probabilistic model should do, and exactly
what a context-free tagger structurally cannot.

## The trellis

The CLI also prints the full dynamic-programming grid — every (word, tag)
cell's best-path log-probability, the same shape as the edit-distance matrix:

```
tag              they      rose
Noun           -11.55    -15.07
Verb           -10.83    -14.74     <- wins
Adjective      -10.42    -19.38
...
```

Reading down the "rose" column, Verb's cell (-14.74) beats Noun's (-15.07) by
a small margin — the visible arithmetic behind the "Verb" decision above.

## Where it falls short

- **The training data is synthetic where it matters most.** The transition
  table is real; the disambiguating emission counts for `rose`, `light`, and
  `still` are hand-picked numbers, not observed frequencies. A tagger trained
  this way "knows" exactly the ambiguities its author thought to seed, and
  not one more. A genuine HMM tagger needs a genuine annotated corpus.
- **Only one word of tag history.** This is a *bigram* HMM: the next tag
  depends on only the immediately preceding tag, the same context-window
  limitation every bigram Markov chain in this repository has. A trigram HMM
  would see further back, at the cost of a much larger transition table.
- **It still doesn't know what words mean.** The model resolves "rose" to
  Verb or Noun by pattern statistics over tags, the same way every other
  counting-based technique here works — not by understanding that one "rose"
  is a flower and the other is a motion. Two completely different ambiguous
  words with identical tag patterns would be resolved identically.
- **Smoothing is uniform and crude.** Add-1 smoothing treats every unseen
  transition and emission as equally unlikely, which is not true — some
  unseen combinations are far more plausible than others. Modern taggers use
  far more sophisticated smoothing, or skip HMMs entirely in favour of
  neural sequence models that learn a continuous representation instead of
  a lookup table.

## Usage

```bash
# Default: "they rose" — the flagship disambiguation, baseline says Noun, HMM says Verb
node hmm-tagger/index.js corpora/sonnets-shakespeare.txt

# The same ambiguous word resolved the other way by different context
node hmm-tagger/index.js corpora/sonnets-shakespeare.txt "the rose is fair"

# A second seeded ambiguity: "light" as verb/adjective vs. the corpus's noun
node hmm-tagger/index.js corpora/sonnets-shakespeare.txt "light the fire"

# Try the contrasting corpus
node hmm-tagger/index.js corpora/sonnets-browning.txt "they rose"
```

The program prints a sample of the trained transition and emission tables
(the data structure), then decodes the given sentence with both the baseline
tagger and Viterbi side by side, flagging every word where they disagree, and
finally prints the full trellis of log-probabilities behind the decision.
